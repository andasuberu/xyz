Shipping Beats Perfect

2026-02-11 • 2 min read

Every designer has a project that's almost finished.

The layout is complete. The typography feels right. The colors are balanced. There's just one more adjustment to make before it's ready.

Then another.

And another.

Weeks later, it's still sitting in a Figma file.

I've been there.

Perfection is appealing because it feels responsible. We tell ourselves we're improving the design, but sometimes we're really protecting ourselves from putting our work in front of people.

The truth is that a design is finished when it solves the problem it set out to solve

It's finished when the end-user... can use it.

Instead of asking, "Is this perfect?" I ask, "Is this useful today?"

A design in the real world is worth more than a flawless mockup that never leaves the canvas.

Ironically, shipping often leads to better design than waiting ever could.

Real users find assumptions you never questioned. They interact with interfaces in unexpected ways. They reveal which problems actually matter and which ones only existed in your head.

No amount of planning can replace real feedback.

That doesn't mean quality is optional.

I still care about thoughtful layouts, visual hierarchy, accessibility, consistency, and attention to detail. Those aren't luxuries—they're part of creating experiences people can rely on.

But there's a difference between delivering something thoughtful and chasing an imaginary version of perfection.

One creates value.

The other creates delay.

Whenever I catch myself endlessly refining something that already works, I try to remember one simple principle:

Ship it.

Then learn.

Then make it better.
